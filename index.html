A read on where the software layer ends and the fleet layer starts for Umibots.

UMIbeach on Valencian beaches. UMIMarquet in Ciutat Vella. A 3-ton pallet AGV into an Inditex platform in Zaragoza. Three product lines, one small software team, one founder wearing four hats. The FCC Medio Ambiente partnership and the move into US and LatAm markets push all of that at once.

The frame

We think about growing software organizations in three layers.

Most small teams let the 10 percent blur into the 30 and the 60, and then every new SKU re-touches every layer. The job is to draw the lines cleanly so the next municipality is a configuration change, not a rewrite.

60%
Traditional code and data

The plumbing that already runs your robots and your traffic controller. Databases, schedulers, dispatch logs, site configs.

30%
Rule-based logic

Routing, dispatch, site-specific constraints, safety envelopes. Deterministic, auditable, and cheap to run.

10%
Genuine machine learning

Perception on UMIbeach. Obstacle avoidance in a live street. The places where probabilistic models actually earn their keep.

The case

One sprint with a product-company CTO. Same axis as yours.

Feeld (Andrew Santus, CTO)

A scoped sprint along the same axis. Workshop, advisory calls, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework.

The output was a shared spec for how internal AI and engineering work gets organized across product lines. Small team, fast decisions, concrete deliverables.

The paper behind it

Interpretable Context Methodology (ACM TiiS). The structural argument: organize the context (perception specs, routing rules, site configs, SKU deltas) as a layered structure so the software team ships consistently to the next municipality without rewriting anything.
